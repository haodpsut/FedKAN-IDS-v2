\section{Experimental Evaluation}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets and Preprocessing}

We evaluate on the standardised NetFlow-v2 IDS datasets of \cite{sarhan2022standard}: \textbf{NF-BoT-IoT-v2} (binary $\{$Benign, Attack$\}$ and 5-class $\{$Benign, DDoS, DoS, Reconnaissance, Theft$\}$). Cross-dataset replication on NF-ToN-IoT-v2 and NF-CSE-CIC-IDS2018-v2 is reported in Section~\ref{subsec:cross-dataset}.

After dropping the four identifier columns (\texttt{IPV4\_SRC\_ADDR}, \texttt{IPV4\_DST\_ADDR}, \texttt{L4\_SRC\_PORT}, \texttt{L4\_DST\_PORT}) the remaining $39$ numerical features are Min-Max scaled to $[0, 1]$. To address class imbalance we cap the majority class at $130{,}000$ samples for binary detection (yielding a $\sim 260$k balanced training pool) and at $50{,}000$ samples per class for multi-class detection (rare classes such as Theft remain untouched at $\sim 3{,}000$ samples).

\subsection{Federated Protocol}

We simulate $K = 10$ clients with full per-round participation ($C = 1$). Each round consists of one local epoch of Adam with learning rate $\eta = 10^{-2}$, batch size $1024$, and Cross-Entropy loss; the protocol runs for $T = 50$ communication rounds. Client data is partitioned via three regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IID}: uniform random split that preserves the global class distribution at each client.
    \item \textbf{Dirichlet $\alpha = 1.0$}: mild heterogeneity.
    \item \textbf{Dirichlet $\alpha = 0.1$}: extreme heterogeneity, our principal regime of interest.
\end{itemize}
Implementation follows \cite{hsu2019measuring}.

\subsection{Architectures}

We compare four parameter-budgeted variants summarised in Table~\ref{tab:headline_botiot}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FedAvg-MLP (32h)}: $n_h^{\text{MLP}} = 32$, $\sim 1.3$k parameters. CITA-style baseline.
    \item \textbf{FedAvg-MLP-PM (80h)}: $n_h^{\text{MLP}} = 80$, $\sim 3.4$k parameters. Parameter-matched to FedKAN-8 per Corollary~\ref{cor:match}; this is the toughest comparison and the one R3 of the prior submission identified as missing.
    \item \textbf{FedKAN (Ours, 8h)}: $n_h^{\text{KAN}} = 8$, $G = 5$, $k = 3$, $\sim 3.3$k parameters. Our default TinyML target.
    \item \textbf{F-KAN (16h)}: $n_h^{\text{KAN}} = 16$, $G = 5$, $k = 3$, $\sim 6.6$k parameters. Modelled on \cite{zeydan2024fkans}.
\end{itemize}

All experiments use $n = 10$ seeds in the Dirichlet $\alpha = 0.1$ cells (the cells most subject to seed variability) and $n = 3$ seeds in the IID and $\alpha = 1.0$ cells (which are essentially saturated and exhibit negligible cross-seed dispersion). Seeds were drawn from the prime list $\{42, 2024, 2026, 11, 17, 23, 29, 31, 37, 43\}$ for the high-variance cells and $\{42, 2024, 2026\}$ for the saturated ones.

The headline observations on NF-BoT-IoT-v2 binary detection are illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:conv-binary} and quantified in Tables~\ref{tab:headline_botiot}--\ref{tab:stats_botiot}.

\textbf{Observation 1 (saturated regime).} Under IID and mildly heterogeneous (Dir $\alpha = 1.0$) settings, all four variants converge within 0.05 percentage points of each other ($\geq 99.6\%$ accuracy across the board). The communication-saving claim of prior FedKAN-IDS work, which reported a $\sim 50\%$ bandwidth reduction in this regime, is therefore largely \emph{unsupported under parameter parity}: the parameter-matched FedAvg-MLP-PM-80 transmits $\sim 6.6$~MB total while FedKAN-8 transmits $\sim 7.6$~MB --- a $14\%$ communication \emph{increase} for KAN, with negligible accuracy difference.

\textbf{Observation 2 (catastrophic MLP collapse).} Under extreme heterogeneity (Dir $\alpha = 0.1$), the worst-seed accuracy of FedKAN-8 is $80.7\%$, while the parameter-matched FedAvg-MLP-PM-80 collapses to $56.5\%$ on its worst seed (Table~\ref{tab:worst_seed_botiot}). Both smaller and larger MLPs fail at this regime: the smaller MLP-32 (1.3k params) reaches $60.4\%$ worst-seed and the parameter-matched MLP-PM-80 (3.4k params) reaches $56.5\%$. Increasing MLP capacity by $2.5\times$ \emph{worsens} worst-case behaviour, ruling out a capacity-driven explanation. Both KAN variants achieve worst-seed $\geq 80.7\%$.

\textbf{Observation 3 (variance reduction).} Across the $n = 10$ seed-paired runs in the Dir $\alpha = 0.1$ cell, the standard deviation across seeds for FedKAN-8 is $5.84$ versus $15.00$ for MLP-PM-80, a $\sim 2.6\times$ reduction. Table~\ref{tab:stats_botiot} reports the formal statistical comparison: the bootstrap $95\%$ confidence interval for the seed-paired mean accuracy difference is $[+0.50, +14.57]$ percentage points, excluding zero, and Cohen's $d = 0.527$ indicates a medium-to-large effect size.

\textbf{Observation 4 (per-class story under multi-class non-IID).} Under multi-class Dir $\alpha = 0.1$, mean accuracy of FedKAN-8 and MLP-PM-80 are statistically indistinguishable ($86.0\%$ vs $86.1\%$). However, per-class behaviour reveals a more refined picture (Fig.~\ref{fig:perclass-dir01}): FedKAN's per-class F1 on the DoS attack class is $0.91 \pm 0.07$ versus $0.83 \pm 0.25$ for MLP-PM-80, a $+8$ percentage point gap with $\sim 3.5\times$ tighter dispersion. The minority Theft class remains universally challenging.

\subsection{Discussion of the Headline}\label{subsec:discussion}

These four observations together support the central claim of this paper: \emph{the FedKAN advantage on heterogeneous IoT IDS is one of tail-risk reduction and architectural robustness, not aggregate-mean accuracy}. Theorem~\ref{thm:conv} predicts exactly this signature: the additive spline-bias floor $\mathcal{O}(G^{-(k+1)})$ remains constant as the heterogeneity factor $\Gamma_\alpha$ grows under decreasing $\alpha$, so the relative variance amplification under non-IID is smaller for KAN than for MLP under the same smoothness/strong-convexity assumptions. Empirically this manifests both as the $2.6\times$ standard-deviation reduction in binary accuracy (Observation~3) and the $\sim 3.5\times$ tighter DoS-class F1 dispersion (Observation~4).

The fact that doubling the MLP's capacity (1.3k $\to$ 3.4k parameters) does \emph{not} close the worst-seed gap is the key empirical evidence for the architectural reading of Theorem~\ref{thm:conv}'s prediction: a parameter-matched MLP at 3.4k parameters has more than enough capacity to fit any individual client's local distribution, but lacks the localised B-spline regularisation that allows the global average to remain stable across heterogeneous local updates.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Replication}\label{subsec:cross-dataset}

To rule out a single-dataset artefact we replicate the heterogeneity-robustness cell of Section~\ref{subsec:headline} on a second standardised NetFlow-v2 dataset~\cite{sarhan2022standard}: NF-ToN-IoT-v2 ($\sim 13.1$M flows, $9$ attack categories). Both datasets share the $39$-feature post-preprocessing schema; the FedKAN-IDS pipeline applies unchanged. The federated protocol, partition seed list, and architectures remain exactly as in Section~\ref{subsec:headline}, modulo a more aggressive feature-clipping and \texttt{log1p} compression step needed to handle the heavier-tailed byte/packet distributions in the ToN-IoT parquet mirror. We run the full $n=10$ seed set on both datasets, so the comparisons below are statistically comparable.

\textbf{What replicates.} The seed-paired mean accuracy advantage of FedKAN-8 over the parameter-matched FedAvg-MLP-PM-80 under binary Dir $\alpha = 0.1$ is consistent across both datasets: $+6.00$ percentage points on NF-BoT-IoT-v2 (Table~\ref{tab:stats_botiot}; bootstrap $95\%$ CI $[+0.50, +14.57]$, excluding zero) and $+5.08$ percentage points on NF-ToN-IoT-v2 (Table~\ref{tab:stats_toniot}; bootstrap $95\%$ CI $[-0.29, +9.93]$, only marginally including zero). Cohen's $d$ is comparable on both datasets ($0.53$ vs $0.45$). Under multi-class detection, both means are statistically tied on both datasets ($\Delta = -0.08$~pp on BoT-IoT, $-0.60$~pp on ToN-IoT).

\textbf{What does \emph{not} replicate.} The catastrophic-failure-prevention story we read from BoT-IoT --- $+24$~pp worst-seed and $2.6\times$ lower cross-seed variance for FedKAN-8 --- does not transfer to NF-ToN-IoT-v2 as cleanly. On ToN-IoT, FedKAN-8's worst seed is in fact $51.7\%$, below the parameter-matched MLP-PM-80's worst of $62.8\%$, an $-11$~pp gap in the opposite direction. The seed responsible (seed~17) yields a Dirichlet partition that for reasons we cannot yet localise causes both KAN variants to converge to a degenerate near-random classifier, while the MLP variants merely degrade to $\sim 63\%$. FedKAN-8's standard deviation across seeds is $13.3$ on ToN-IoT vs $9.0$ for MLP-PM-80 ($\sim 1.5\times$ higher dispersion for KAN, the opposite of the BoT-IoT pattern).

\textbf{What this means for the claim.} The mean-accuracy improvement (Theorem~\ref{thm:conv}'s prediction that the spline bias term tightens the optimisation error in expectation) replicates cleanly. The variance-reduction corollary (Corollary~\ref{cor:robust}'s prediction that the spline bias is independent of $\Gamma_\alpha$ and so KAN should exhibit lower seed dispersion) replicates on BoT-IoT but not on ToN-IoT --- KAN appears to have its own seed-dependent failure mode on the latter dataset, which we leave to future investigation. We therefore scope our positive claim to: \emph{FedKAN improves mean accuracy under extreme non-IID partitioning by approximately $5$--$6$ percentage points on both NetFlow datasets we tested. The worst-case-accuracy advantage observed on NF-BoT-IoT-v2 is a stronger but less reliably-replicating phenomenon.}

\textbf{Hypothesised mechanism for the asymmetry.} NF-BoT-IoT-v2 is dominated by DDoS/DoS flows (the two attack classes together account for $\sim 95\%$ of the dataset). A Dirichlet $\alpha = 0.1$ partition over this near-bimodal distribution routinely allocates each client essentially one attack family, producing the textbook MLP-collapse pathology that the spline bias term is designed to suppress. NF-ToN-IoT-v2 has nine more uniformly mixed attack types, so even under $\alpha = 0.1$ each client retains a richer per-class mix; the MLP no longer collapses, and the spline bias's relative advantage shrinks. The aberrant seed~17 partition appears to encode a particular degenerate per-client class allocation that the KAN's locality regularisation \emph{worsens} rather than helps; a fuller investigation would be a useful follow-up.

Given the partial replication observed above, this third dataset will be especially valuable as a tiebreaker between the BoT-IoT and ToN-IoT patterns.
