\subsection{Datasets and Preprocessing}
We evaluate on the standardised NetFlow-v2 IDS datasets of \cite{sarhan2022standard}: NF-BoT-IoT-v2 (binary $\{$Benign, Attack$\}$ and 5-class), and (in cross-dataset replication) NF-ToN-IoT-v2 and NF-CSE-CIC-IDS2018-v2. After dropping the four identifier columns (\texttt{IPV4\_SRC\_ADDR}, \texttt{IPV4\_DST\_ADDR}, \texttt{L4\_SRC\_PORT}, \texttt{L4\_DST\_PORT}) the remaining 39 numerical features are Min-Max scaled to $[0, 1]$.

\subsection{Federated Protocol and Architectures}
We simulate $K=10$ clients with full per-round participation. Each round consists of one local epoch of Adam with learning rate $\eta=10^{-2}$, batch size 1024, and Cross-Entropy loss; we run $T=50$ rounds total. Client data is partitioned via three regimes: IID (uniform random split), Dirichlet $\alpha=1.0$ (mild heterogeneity), and Dirichlet $\alpha=0.1$ (extreme heterogeneity), implemented as in \cite{hsu2019measuring}.

We compare four parameter-budgeted architectures, summarised in Table \ref{tab:headline_botiot}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{FedAvg-MLP (32h)}: $n_h^{\text{MLP}}=32$, $\sim 1.3$k parameters (CITA-style baseline).
\item \textbf{FedAvg-MLP-PM (80h)}: $n_h^{\text{MLP}}=80$, $\sim 3.4$k parameters (parameter-matched to FedKAN-8 per Corollary \ref{cor:match}).
\item \textbf{FedKAN (Ours, 8h)}: $n_h^{\text{KAN}}=8$, $G=5$, $k=3$, $\sim 3.3$k parameters (TinyML target).
\item \textbf{F-KAN (16h)}: $n_h^{\text{KAN}}=16$, $G=5$, $k=3$, $\sim 6.6$k parameters (Zeydan-style deeper KAN \cite{zeydan2024fkans}).
\end{itemize}

The headline observations on NF-BoT-IoT-v2 (binary, $n=10$ seeds) are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Under IID and mildly heterogeneous (Dir $\alpha=1.0$) settings, all four variants converge within $0.05$ percentage points of each other ($\geq 99.6\%$ accuracy across the board).
\item Under extreme heterogeneity (Dir $\alpha=0.1$), \textbf{FedKAN-8 achieves a worst-seed accuracy of $80.7\%$, while the parameter-matched MLP-PM-80 collapses to $56.5\%$ on its worst seed.} Smaller and larger MLPs both fail at this regime, ruling out a capacity explanation: the small MLP-32 (1.3k params) reaches $60.4\%$ worst-seed and the parameter-matched MLP-PM-80 (3.4k params) reaches $56.5\%$ — increasing capacity by $2.5\times$ does not improve worst-case behaviour.
\item Across the $n=10$ seed-paired runs, the bootstrap $95\%$ CI for the FedKAN-8 minus MLP-PM-80 mean accuracy difference is $[+0.50, +14.57]$ percentage points; the standard deviation across seeds for FedKAN-8 ($5.84$) is $\sim 2.6\times$ smaller than for MLP-PM-80 ($15.00$).
\item Under multi-class Dir $\alpha=0.1$, the mean accuracy of FedKAN-8 and MLP-PM-80 are statistically indistinguishable ($86.0\%$ vs $86.1\%$), but FedKAN's per-class F1 on the DoS class is $0.91 \pm 0.08$ versus $0.83 \pm 0.25$ for MLP-PM-80, a $+8$ percentage point gap with $\sim 3\times$ tighter dispersion.
\end{enumerate}

These observations together support the claim that FedKAN's advantage is one of \emph{tail-risk reduction} and \emph{architectural robustness} rather than aggregate-mean accuracy improvement. Theorem \ref{thm:conv} predicts exactly this signature: the spline-bias floor remains constant while $\Gamma_\alpha$ grows, so the relative variance amplification under non-IID is smaller for KAN than for MLP under the same assumptions.
