\begin{abstract}
Federated Learning (FL) for IoT intrusion detection (IDS) is bottlenecked by both the bandwidth budget of edge devices and the accuracy degradation that occurs when client traffic distributions are heterogeneous. Recent work has integrated Kolmogorov--Arnold Networks (KANs) into FL outside the IDS setting; we present \textbf{FedKAN-IDS}, the first systematic, parameter-matched empirical study of Federated KANs on three standardised NetFlow-v2 intrusion detection datasets (NF-BoT-IoT-v2, NF-ToN-IoT-v2, NF-CSE-CIC-IDS2018-v2), each evaluated under three data-heterogeneity regimes and both binary and multi-class detection. Our contribution is empirical rather than methodological, and we are careful to scope it accordingly. \textbf{(i)} Under extreme client heterogeneity (Dirichlet $\alpha = 0.1$) FedKAN-8 ($\sim 3.3$k parameters) achieves a consistent $+2$ to $+6$ percentage-point mean-accuracy advantage over a parameter-matched FedAvg-MLP across all three datasets, with the magnitude tracking the underlying dataset's class-distribution skew. \textbf{(ii)} On the most skewed dataset (BoT-IoT-v2) the advantage extends to a $+24$ pp worst-seed accuracy gap and a $2.6\times$ variance reduction; doubling MLP capacity does not close the gap, supporting an architectural rather than capacity-driven reading. \textbf{(iii)} On the less skewed datasets (ToN-IoT-v2, CSE-CIC-IDS2018-v2) the worst-seed advantage diminishes or, in one seed of ToN-IoT, inverts --- a finding we report transparently and discuss in terms of when FedKAN's spline-locality regulariser does and does not help. We anchor these observations in a convergence analysis showing that the KAN B-spline approximation introduces an additive $\mathcal{O}(G^{-(k+1)})$ bias term that is independent of the data-heterogeneity factor $\Gamma_\alpha$ and so behaves as an effective regulariser whose relative impact tracks the underlying heterogeneity. Code, configurations, and 170 reproducible runs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}
