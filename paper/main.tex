\author{

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Federated Learning (FL) for IoT intrusion detection systems (IDS) faces a fundamental tension between the bandwidth budget of edge devices and the catastrophic accuracy degradation that dense neural network architectures exhibit when client data is heterogeneously distributed. We present \textbf{FedKAN-IDS}, the first systematic study of Federated Kolmogorov--Arnold Networks (KANs) on NetFlow-based IoT intrusion detection, with a contribution that is empirical rather than methodological: we show that under extreme client heterogeneity (Dirichlet $\alpha = 0.1$), the worst-seed binary detection accuracy of FedKAN-8 ($\sim 3.3$k parameters) is $80.7\%$, while a parameter-matched FedAvg-MLP at $\sim 3.4$k parameters drops to $56.5\%$ on the same partition. Doubling MLP capacity does not close this gap, indicating an architectural rather than capacity-driven advantage. The seed-paired bootstrap $95\%$ confidence interval for the FedKAN--MLP-PM accuracy difference is $[+0.50, +14.57]$ percentage points across $n=10$ seeds, and FedKAN's per-class F1 on the DoS attack class is $+8$ percentage points higher than the parameter-matched MLP under multi-class non-IID. We anchor the empirical finding in a convergence analysis showing that the KAN B-spline approximation introduces an additive $\mathcal{O}(G^{-(k+1)})$ bias term that is independent of the data-heterogeneity factor $\Gamma_\alpha$, behaving as an effective regulariser. Code, configurations, and 146 reproducible runs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}
